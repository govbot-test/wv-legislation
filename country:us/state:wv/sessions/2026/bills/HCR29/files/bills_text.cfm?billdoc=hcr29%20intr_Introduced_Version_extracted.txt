Howell, Anders, Kelly, Ferrell, Petitto, Adkins, Drennan, Anderson, Dean, Dillon, Dittman, Hillenbrand, Hite, Horst, Hott, Jennings, Lucas, Mallow, Martin, Masters, Miller, Parsons, Phillips, Pritt, D. Smith, Stephens, Vance, G. Ward, White, Willis, Worrell, and Zatezalo)[Introduced March 5, 2026; referred to the Committee on Rules] Requesting the Joint Committee on Government and Finance study the feasibility of establishing a flexible, short-duration, on-demand respite care program for seniors and caregivers in West Virginia.Whereas, West Virginia has one of the oldest populations in the nation, with a growing number of families serving as primary caregivers for elderly spouses and relatives who require supervision or limited assistance but do not yet require full-time in-home services; andWhereas, Existing state-supported programs, including the Lighthouse Program and the Medicaid Aged and Disabled Waiver, generally provide services in structured weekly blocks with minimum hour thresholds that may exceed the intermittent needs of many caregivers; andWhereas, Many caregivers require only short-duration assistance for events such as medical appointments, necessary travel, or personal obligations, often with modest advance notice; andWhereas, In the absence of flexible short-term respite options, families may feel compelled to accept larger weekly service packages than are necessary, resulting in avoidable costs to both the family and, in Medicaid-supported cases, the State of West Virginia; andWhereas, The average annual cost of nursing home care significantly exceeds the cost of limited in-home assistance, and premature institutional placement increases long-term Medicaid expenditures borne by taxpayers; andWhereas, Providing limited, flexible respite coverage may prevent or delay nursing home placement, thereby reducing overall state Medicaid spending while allowing seniors to remain in their homes with dignity; andWhereas, Rural counties in West Virginia are disproportionately affected by this service gap due to limited provider availability, workforce shortages, transportation barriers, and fewer private-pay alternatives, leaving families with fewer options and higher relative burdens; therefore, be itResolved by the Legislature of West Virginia:That the Joint Committee on Government and Finance is hereby requested to study the feasibility of creating a flexible, on-demand respite care program allowing eligible seniors and caregivers to schedule short-duration assistance with reasonable advance notice, such as one week, for limited hours as needed; and, be itFurther Resolved, That the study examine:1. The comparative cost of flexible respite care versus nursing home placement, including long-term Medicaid expenditures;2. The potential savings associated with delaying institutionalization through targeted short-term support;3. Whether existing programs may be modified to allow hourly or fractional utilization without mandatory weekly minimums;4. The feasibility of a pilot voucher-based or registry-based caregiver pool administered through county aging providers, particularly in rural counties;5. Funding mechanisms, including potential Medicaid waivers, federal participation, or targeted state appropriations;6. Workforce capacity in rural areas and strategies to recruit and retain part-time respite providers; and 7. Appropriate oversight and accountability measures to ensure fiscal responsibility and prevent abuse; and, be itFurther Resolved, That the committee report its findings, conclusions, and any recommended legislation before the next regular session of the Legislature.
